# WHO Guidelines for Drinking-water Quality
## Fourth Edition, 2022 Update | World Health Organization

This document summarises WHO drinking water quality guidelines relevant
to Indian water quality assessment. Where WHO values differ from BIS
IS 10500:2012, both are noted. WaterSentinel uses BIS standards as
primary reference for Indian water but references WHO for health context.

---

## 1. WHO Framework Approach

WHO guidelines are health-based targets, not regulatory standards.
They represent the concentration of a substance that does not result
in significant health risk to a person consuming 2 litres of water
per day over a lifetime.

**Key distinction from BIS:** WHO guidelines are global health benchmarks.
BIS IS 10500 is India's legally applicable standard. For WaterSentinel,
BIS IS 10500 is the primary reference. WHO provides the health science
basis for understanding risk.

---

## 2. Microbial Quality — Highest Priority

### 2.1 WHO Position on Microbial Risk
Microbial contamination is the primary concern in developing countries.
WHO states: "The most common and widespread health risk associated with
drinking water is microbial contamination."

- **E. coli / thermotolerant coliforms:** Must not be detectable in
  any 100 mL sample. This aligns with BIS IS 10500.
- **Cryptosporidium and Giardia:** Not removed by chlorination alone.
  Require filtration. Relevant for open wells and hand pumps.

### 2.2 Risk by Source Type (WHO Assessment)
- **Piped treated supply:** Low risk if treatment maintained
- **Borewell (protected):** Low-medium risk if properly cased
- **Hand pump:** Medium risk — shared infrastructure, periodic contamination
- **Open well:** High risk — surface water entry, animal access
- **Unprotected spring:** Very high risk

## 3. Chemical Parameters — WHO vs BIS Comparison

### 3.1 Arsenic
- **WHO guideline:** 0.01 mg/L
- **BIS IS 10500:** 0.01 mg/L (same)
- **Health basis:** Carcinogenic. Causes skin lesions, bladder, lung,
  kidney cancers. No safe threshold identified.

### 3.2 Fluoride
- **WHO guideline:** 1.5 mg/L
- **BIS IS 10500:** Acceptable 1.0 mg/L, Permissible 1.5 mg/L
- **Health basis:** Below 0.5 mg/L: dental caries risk.
  1.5–4.0 mg/L: dental fluorosis.
  Above 4.0 mg/L: skeletal fluorosis.
- **WHO note:** In areas with high natural fluoride (like Nalgonda),
  WHO recommends defluoridation as a community-level intervention.

### 3.3 Nitrate
- **WHO guideline:** 50 mg/L (as NO3) = 11 mg/L as NO3-N
- **BIS IS 10500:** 45 mg/L
- **Health basis:** Methaemoglobinaemia in infants. Possible colorectal
  cancer link in adults with long-term exposure.

### 3.4 Lead
- **WHO guideline:** 0.01 mg/L (provisional — no safe level)
- **BIS IS 10500:** 0.01 mg/L
- **Health basis:** Neurotoxic. No safe level especially in children.
  Source: lead solder in old plumbing, lead pipes in pre-1990 buildings.
- **WaterSentinel relevance:** Old buildings in Hyderabad and Vijayawada
  city centres may have lead plumbing. Metallic taste + old building
  = recommend lead test.

### 3.5 Iron
- **WHO guideline:** No health-based guideline (health risk at levels
  found in drinking water is low)
- **BIS IS 10500:** 0.3 mg/L (aesthetic/practical limit)
- **WHO note:** Iron causes aesthetic problems (taste, staining) at
  levels above 0.3 mg/L but is not a direct health hazard at these
  concentrations. However, iron promotes bacterial growth.

### 3.6 Manganese
- **WHO guideline:** 0.08 mg/L (health-based, 2022 update)
- **BIS IS 10500:** Acceptable 0.1 mg/L, Permissible 0.3 mg/L
- **WHO note:** 2022 update lowered the guideline from 0.4 mg/L to
  0.08 mg/L based on new neurological evidence. BIS IS 10500 is
  less stringent than current WHO guidance.

### 3.7 TDS
- **WHO guideline:** No health-based guideline
- **BIS IS 10500:** 500 mg/L acceptable, 2000 mg/L permissible
- **WHO note:** TDS affects taste palatability. Above 1000 mg/L most
  people find water unpalatable. WHO notes high TDS is linked to
  scale formation and aesthetic issues but does not set a health limit.

### 3.8 Hydrogen Sulphide (H2S)
- **WHO guideline:** No formal guideline (concentration in drinking
  water rarely reaches health-relevant levels)
- **BIS IS 10500:** 0.05 mg/L
- **WHO note:** H2S is detectable by odour at extremely low concentrations
  (0.0005 mg/L). The health threshold is much higher than the odour
  threshold. H2S in drinking water is primarily an aesthetic problem,
  not a health emergency at typical groundwater concentrations.

## 4. WHO Safe Water System (SWS) Principles

Relevant for WaterSentinel's advisory generation:

### 4.1 Multiple Barrier Approach
WHO recommends protecting drinking water quality at multiple points:
1. Source protection (keeping contamination out of the source)
2. Treatment (removing contamination at household or community level)
3. Safe storage (preventing recontamination after treatment)

**WaterSentinel application:** For each water source type, the advisory
should address all three barriers, not just treatment.

### 4.2 Household Water Treatment (HWT) Options — WHO Assessed

| Method | Bacteria | Viruses | Protozoa | Chemical removal | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Boiling | ✅ Effective | ✅ Effective | ✅ Effective | ❌ None | Very low |
| Chlorination | ✅ Effective | ✅ Effective | ❌ Limited | ❌ None | Low |
| UV purifier | ✅ Effective | ✅ Effective | ✅ Effective | ❌ None | Medium |
| Ceramic filter | ✅ Good | ⚠️ Partial | ✅ Good | ❌ None | Low |
| RO system | ✅ Good | ✅ Good | ✅ Good | ✅ Effective | High |
| Iron removal filter | ❌ None | ❌ None | ❌ None | ✅ Iron only | Medium |

**WaterSentinel advisory rule:** Match treatment to contaminant.
UV treats microbial. RO treats chemical + microbial. Iron filter
treats iron only. Never recommend UV for iron contamination.

## 5. WHO on Safe Water for Specific Uses

### 5.1 Drinking
Use WHO/BIS limits as specified above.

### 5.2 Bathing and Personal Hygiene
WHO states that water used for bathing need not meet drinking water
standards. Microbial contamination at levels present in groundwater
is generally not a health risk for bathing in healthy adults.
Exception: open wounds, infants, immunocompromised individuals.

**WaterSentinel application:** Water with H2S (egg smell) or iron
contamination is SAFE for bathing even without treatment. This is an
important advisory message to prevent unnecessary alarm.

### 5.3 Cooking
Cooking water should meet drinking water standards. Boiling eliminates
microbial risk but does not remove chemical contaminants (TDS, fluoride,
iron, nitrate). For chemical contamination, RO water should be used
for cooking as well as drinking.
