# India Water Sources Guide
## Urban and Peri-Urban Water Supply in Indian Cities
## Custom Authored for WaterSentinel | June 2026

This document describes the four water source types used in Indian cities
and peri-urban areas. WaterSentinel's SourceSense agent uses this guide
to understand source-specific contamination risks, maintenance needs,
and escalation pathways.

---

## 1. Borewell (Groundwater — Personal/RWA)

### 1.1 What It Is
A borewell (also called tubewell) is a vertical hole drilled into the
ground to access groundwater. In Indian cities, borewells are drilled
by individual homeowners, apartment complexes, or RWAs at personal
expense (₹1–3 lakhs typically).

### 1.2 Typical Depths by Geology

| Geology | Location Examples | Typical Depth | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Alluvial soil | Vijayawada, Guntur, coastal AP | 50–150 feet | Shallower, higher TDS |
| Weathered granite | Bangalore outer areas | 100–200 feet | Iron risk |
| Deccan Trap basalt | Hyderabad, Pune | 150–350 feet | H2S + Iron risk |
| Hard rock / Deccan deep | BHEL, Nallagandla, Tellapur | 300–500 feet | High H2S risk |
| Laterite (coastal) | Mangalore, Goa, Kerala | 30–80 feet | Iron risk |
| Eastern Ghats granite | Visakhapatnam hilly areas | 100–250 feet | Iron, Fluoride |

### 1.3 Ownership and Responsibility
- **Individual borewell:** Homeowner's personal infrastructure. Owner
  has full responsibility for water quality. No government oversight.
  Owner can afford to act (already invested ₹1–3 lakhs).
- **RWA/Society borewell:** Managed by RWA committee. Shared cost.
  Requires committee decision to treat or test.

### 1.4 Common Contamination by Depth
- **<100 feet:** Higher bacterial risk (surface contamination reaches
  shallow aquifer). Lower mineral contamination.
- **100–250 feet:** Iron and manganese common. Moderate bacterial risk.
- **250–400 feet:** H2S very common (anaerobic zone). Iron common.
  Bacterial risk low (depth acts as natural filter).
- **>400 feet:** H2S highest. Fluoride possible in certain geologies.
  Very low bacterial risk.

### 1.5 Seasonal Behaviour
- Pre-monsoon: Water table drops. May need to deepen borewell.
  Contaminant concentrations increase.
- Post-monsoon: Water table rises. Quality generally improves.
  Shallow borewells may show brief microbial spike from surface runoff.

### 1.6 Maintenance Requirements
- **Annual:** Motor/pump servicing
- **Every 3 years:** Borewell cleaning and flushing
- **Continuous:** Check for surface water entry at borewell head
  (should be cemented and sealed)
- **Signs of problem:** Reduced yield (lower water pressure), sudden
  change in colour or smell, motor running but low output

### 1.7 WaterSentinel Action for Borewell Reports
- Personal advisory (not municipal escalation — borewell is private)
- Filter recommendation specific to contaminant type
- Nearest NABL-certified water testing lab
- CGWB helpline for aquifer assessment (free service)

## 2. Municipal Pipeline (Surface Water — Government)

### 2.1 What It Is
Municipal pipeline supply is treated water from a river or reservoir,
distributed through a network of pipes by the city water board. In
Hyderabad: HMWSSB. In Vijayawada: VWSS. In Bangalore: BWSSB.

### 2.2 The One-Hour Supply Reality
In most Indian cities, municipal pipeline supply is available for
approximately ONE HOUR per day, typically in the early morning
(5:00 AM – 7:00 AM). Residents must store this supply for daily use.

**Standard storage solution:**
Most households and apartments have dug underground cement storage tanks
(sump tanks) of 1,000–2,000 litre capacity. Water collected during
the supply hour is stored and used throughout the day.

**This storage step is a major contamination risk point:**
- Bacteria can multiply in stored water over 12–24 hours
- Unclean tank walls harbour biofilm
- If tank lid is not sealed, insects, lizards, and debris enter
- In multi-storey buildings, water is pumped from underground sump
  to rooftop overhead tank (OHT) — double storage = double risk

### 2.3 Quality at Different Points

| Point | Quality | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Treatment plant outlet | Good — BIS compliant | Chlorinated and filtered |
| Distribution main | Good if pipe is intact | Degrades in old/damaged pipes |
| Colony supply pipe | Variable | Depends on pipe age and maintenance |
| Underground sump | Variable | Depends on tank cleaning frequency |
| Overhead tank | Variable | Secondary storage = additional risk |
| Tap | Variable | Final quality the resident experiences |

### 2.4 Tank Cleaning — Critical Maintenance
**Recommended frequency:** Every 6 months
**Method:** Empty tank completely. Scrub walls with brush. Apply
bleaching powder solution (200g bleaching powder dissolved in 10L water
per 1000L tank capacity). Leave for 30 minutes. Rinse and refill.

**Warning signs that tank needs cleaning:**
- Musty smell from water
- Green/black growth visible on tank walls
- Sediment or silt at bottom
- Water tastes different from usual

### 2.5 Sewage Back-Siphoning — Serious Risk
When municipal pipeline pressure drops (common during low-supply periods
or pump failures), a vacuum can form in the pipe. If there is any
crack or cross-connection near a sewer line, sewage can be drawn into
the water pipe. This is a documented cause of waterborne disease outbreaks
in Indian cities.

**Signs:** Sudden appearance of very dark, smelly water from the tap
after a period of no supply. This is an EMERGENCY situation.

### 2.6 WaterSentinel Action for Municipal Pipeline Reports
- Identify if issue is building-level (tank) or supply-level (pipeline)
- Building-level: Tank cleaning advisory
- Supply-level: Generate HMWSSB/VWSS complaint via ActionBridge MCP
- Community cluster: Escalate as ward-level supply issue

## 3. Hand Pump (Groundwater — Shared Community)

### 3.1 What It Is
Hand pumps are manually operated pumps installed on community borewells.
The most common type in India is the India Mark II deep-well hand pump,
standardised by the Government of India. Typically found in:
- Peri-urban low-income areas
- Government-installed community water points
- Rural habitations

### 3.2 Typical Depth and Geology
- Standard India Mark II: 30–45 metres (100–150 feet)
- Designed for shallow to medium aquifers
- Common in alluvial plains, sandy soils

### 3.3 Contamination Risk Profile
**Higher risk than private borewells because:**
- Shared use by many families — higher potential for contamination
  introduction at pump platform
- No individual ownership = collective action problem for maintenance
- Platform drainage often inadequate — surface water pools near pump
  and can enter aquifer
- Regular maintenance (gasket, handle, cylinder) often delayed due to
  shared responsibility

**Common contamination issues:**
- Bacterial contamination (most common) — from poor platform drainage
- Iron — from aquifer characteristics
- Nitrate — near agricultural areas, from runoff entering around pump

### 3.4 Seasonal Risk
Post-monsoon: Highest risk period. Flooding can directly contaminate
the aquifer through the pump platform.

### 3.5 WaterSentinel Action for Hand Pump Reports
- Community advisory (not personal — shared infrastructure)
- Escalate to Panchayat/Ward office for maintenance
- Recommend community chlorination if bacterial contamination suspected
- Generate GHMC/Panchayat complaint via ActionBridge

## 4. Open Well (Surface/Shallow Groundwater)

### 4.1 What It Is
Open wells are manually dug wells, typically 5–30 feet deep and 3–6
feet in diameter. Common in:
- Rural areas
- Older peri-urban areas
- Agricultural communities

### 4.2 Contamination Risk — Always Assume High
Open wells have the highest contamination risk of all four source types:
- Open to atmosphere — insects, birds, animals can enter
- Sunlight exposure promotes algae growth
- Shallow depth — surface contamination easily reaches water table
- No casing — borewall contamination entry
- Post-monsoon flooding frequently contaminates open wells directly

**WaterSentinel rule:** For open well reports, always include bacterial
contamination warning regardless of stated symptoms.

### 4.3 Absolutely Do Not Drink Without Treatment
Open well water should NEVER be consumed without treatment:
- Minimum: Boil vigorously for 1 minute
- Better: UV purifier
- Best: RO + UV combination

### 4.4 Treatment That Works for Open Wells
- **Household chlorination:** Add 2.5ml of bleach (5% chlorine) per
  1000L of water. Wait 30 minutes. Safe to drink.
- **Bleaching powder:** 2.5g per 1000L. Effective for bacterial contamination.
- **Alum flocculation:** Settle turbidity before chlorination.

### 4.5 WaterSentinel Action for Open Well Reports
- Always issue bacterial contamination advisory regardless of symptoms
- Recommend treatment method above
- If multiple households depending on open well: escalate to Panchayat
  for covered well construction or piped supply request

## 5. Comparative Risk Summary

| Source Type | Bacterial Risk | Chemical Risk | User Agency | Escalation Path |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Borewell (personal) | Low-Medium | Medium-High | High (owns it) | Personal action |
| Municipal pipeline | Low (treated) | Low (pipe issues) | Medium | HMWSSB/VWSS |
| Hand pump | Medium-High | Medium | Low (shared) | Panchayat/Ward |
| Open well | Very High | Low-Medium | Low-Medium | Panchayat |
