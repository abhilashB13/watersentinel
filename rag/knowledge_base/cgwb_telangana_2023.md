# CGWB Groundwater Quality — Telangana State
## Central Ground Water Board, South Eastern Regional Office | 2023

This document summarises groundwater quality findings for Telangana state
from CGWB monitoring data. Used by WaterSentinel's WaterProfiler agent to
provide location-specific context when diagnosing water quality in Hyderabad
and Telangana districts.

---

## 1. Overview

Telangana has approximately 79,000 functional borewells across the state.
Groundwater is the primary source of drinking water for approximately 70%
of Telangana's population. The geology of Telangana is predominantly
Deccan Trap basalt in the north-west and Eastern Ghats granite/gneiss in
the east, with alluvial deposits along river valleys.

---

## 2. Hyderabad Metropolitan Area — Groundwater Profile

### 2.1 Geology
Hyderabad and its surrounding districts (Ranga Reddy, Medchal-Malkajgiri,
Sangareddy) sit on Deccan Trap basalt and granite basement rock.
Borewell depths in this geology:
- Weathered zone: 30–80 feet — highest contamination risk
- Fractured basalt: 100–250 feet — common residential borewell depth
- Deep fractured zone: 300–500 feet — required in western corridor
  (Nallagandla, BHEL, Kondapur, Gachibowli, Miyapur, Tellapur)

### 2.2 Key Contamination Issues — Ranga Reddy District

**Iron (Fe):**
- Exceeds BIS limit of 0.3 mg/L in approximately 35% of tested borewells
  in Ranga Reddy district
- Primary areas affected: Nallagandla, Gachibowli, Kondapur, BHEL colony,
  Miyapur, Tellapur, Osman Nagar
- Cause: Iron-rich basalt and laterite deposits in the weathered zone
- Depth correlation: Most severe in borewells 150–300 feet tapping
  the weathered basalt zone

**Hydrogen Sulphide (H2S):**
- Egg smell reported widely in deep borewells (300–500 feet)
- Primary cause: Anaerobic conditions at depth support sulphur-reducing
  bacteria (Desulfovibrio species) which produce H2S
- Affected areas: BHEL MIG/LIG/HIG colonies, Nallagandla, Tellapur,
  Osman Nagar, Narsingi
- Depth correlation: Almost exclusively in borewells deeper than 250 feet
  in Deccan Plateau formations

**TDS:**
- Above BIS acceptable limit (500 mg/L) in approximately 28% of Ranga
  Reddy borewells
- Hyderabad western corridor (Gachibowli, Madhapur, Kondapur) has
  relatively lower TDS due to recent hard-rock formations
- Hyderabad eastern areas (LB Nagar, Uppal, Nacharam) show higher TDS
  due to older alluvial deposits near Musi river

## 3. Nalgonda District — Fluoride Crisis

Nalgonda is one of the most severely fluoride-affected districts in India.

**Fluoride levels:** 1.5–20 mg/L in affected habitations (BIS limit: 1.0 mg/L)
**Affected population:** Approximately 1 million people
**Health impact documented:** Dental fluorosis in 52% of children,
skeletal fluorosis in adults in severely affected villages
**Depth correlation:** Fluoride concentrations increase with borewell depth
in Nalgonda, opposite to most other contaminants
**Status:** Government defluoridation plants installed but maintenance
is inconsistent. Many residents still consume fluoride-contaminated water.

---

## 4. Khammam and Warangal Districts — Mixed Contamination

- Iron above BIS limits in alluvial areas near Godavari tributaries
- Nitrate above limits near agricultural zones (sugarcane, cotton)
- TDS above acceptable limits in southern Khammam near Telangana-AP border
- Manganese detected above limits in some Warangal borewells (0.2–0.5 mg/L)

---

## 5. Medchal-Malkajgiri District (North Hyderabad)

- Rapid urbanisation has increased nitrate contamination from septic tanks
- Iron contamination in western Medchal areas
- Municipal supply increasingly supplemented by treated Godavari water
  (Godavari Drinking Water Supply Project) which has better quality

## 6. Seasonal Variation — Critical for WaterSentinel

**Pre-monsoon (March–June):**
- Water table drops, contaminant concentrations increase
- H2S and iron concentrations typically highest
- Fluoride risk increases in already-affected areas
- Most critical period for water quality advisories

**Post-monsoon (October–December):**
- Water table recharges, dilution effect reduces some contaminants
- BUT: Surface contamination enters shallow borewells and hand pumps
- Microbial contamination risk peaks post-monsoon
- Open wells and hand pumps most vulnerable

**Winter (December–February):**
- Most stable water quality period
- Lower microbial contamination
- Chemical contaminants at moderate levels

**WaterSentinel application:** Month of report submission affects
probability weighting of certain contaminants.

## 7. Municipal Supply Quality (HMWSSB)

HMWSSB (Hyderabad Metropolitan Water Supply and Sewerage Board) sources
water from:
- Manjira reservoir (western supply)
- Krishna river (Phase I, II, III projects)
- Godavari river (ongoing expansion)

HMWSSB treated water at supply point generally meets BIS IS 10500 standards.
Quality degradation occurs:
1. In the distribution network (old pipes, cross-connections)
2. In household underground storage tanks (bacterial growth if not cleaned)
3. During pressure fluctuations (sewage back-siphoning risk)

**Known issues:**
- Underground tank contamination: Most common reason for degraded
  municipal supply quality at tap level
- Recommendation: Clean underground tanks every 6 months with bleaching
  powder solution (200g per 1000L tank capacity)
